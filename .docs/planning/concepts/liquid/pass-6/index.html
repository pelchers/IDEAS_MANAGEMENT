The Scribe

Your calligraphy research and creative writing assistant

The Scribe 10:15 AM

Good morning, Eleanor. I have reviewed the Harrison-Nakamura commission brief. The request to blend Copperplate with Kaisho elements is a fascinating cross-cultural challenge. Shall I research historical examples of Western-Japanese calligraphic fusion?

10:18 AM Eleanor

Yes, that would be wonderful. Also, can you suggest which nib would best handle both the hairline upstrokes of Copperplate and the broader strokes needed for Kanji characters? I'm torn between the Nikko G and something with more flex.

The Scribe 10:20 AM

For this dual-script commission, I would recommend the Zebra G (Titanium) as your primary nib. Here is my reasoning:

  • Flex range: It offers moderate flex sufficient for Copperplate shade/hairline contrast without being as soft as a vintage Gillott 303.
  • Durability: The titanium coating means it withstands the higher pressure needed for broader Kaisho strokes without splaying prematurely.
  • Ink flow: Excellent ink reservoir for sustained strokes, critical when forming multi-stroke Kanji characters.

For the finest hairlines on the English text, you could switch to the Nikko G for those passages specifically. A two-nib approach would give you the best of both worlds.

10:24 AM Eleanor

That makes perfect sense. What about ink compatibility? Will Sumi ink work well in the Zebra G, or should I thin it slightly? I usually use it full strength for brush work.

The Scribe 10:26 AM

Excellent question. Sumi ink #6000 at full concentration tends to be too viscous for pointed pen use -- it can cause skipping and buildup between the tines. I recommend:

  1. Dilute the sumi ink approximately 15-20% with distilled water
  2. Add 1 drop of gum arabic per 10ml to maintain opacity while improving flow
  3. Test on a scrap of the actual Crane Lettra stock before committing to the final pieces

The Finetec Arabic Gold, by contrast, works beautifully with the Zebra G straight from the palette -- just activate it with a few drops of water and load the nib generously.

10:30 AM Eleanor

Perfect. One more thing -- the client's grandmother's family name is Murakami. Can you show me the correct stroke order for the Kanji characters so I get the brush direction right?

The Scribe 10:32 AM

Of course. 村上 (Murakami) consists of two characters:

村 (mura, village) -- 7 strokes. Begin with the left-falling stroke of the tree radical, then the horizontal, the vertical, and complete the right portion left-to-right, top-to-bottom.

上 (kami/ue, above) -- 3 strokes. One of the simplest kanji: short vertical, long horizontal, bottom horizontal. In Kaisho style, keep strokes deliberate with clear start/stop points.

Shall I compose a practice layout showing both characters at the scale you will use on the place cards?
